The Magic of Thinking Big

David Joseph Schwartz

★★★★★ 5 stars
Mar 2019
Synthesis

Schwartz's core argument is that the size of your success is directly proportional to the size of your belief. The book emphasizes that self-doubt is your greatest enemy—you must believe in yourself before anyone else will. The patterns across your highlights reveal that confidence is built through action (not waiting for motivation), fear is conquered by doing (not deliberating), and how you think about yourself literally shapes how others treat you. The practical takeaways are clear: visualize what can be, not what is; surround yourself with ambitious people; treat everyone as important; focus on big objectives instead of surface-level problems; and invest relentlessly in your own growth.

Key Insights
  • Belief precedes ability—the 'how-to-do-it' only develops after you believe 'I-can-do-it'
  • Action is the antidote to fear; indecision fertilizes it
  • You are measured by the size of your thinking, not your credentials
  • Your environment shapes your mindset—curate it deliberately
  • Treat every person as the most important person on earth

The Power of Your Subconscious Mind

Joseph Murphy

★★★★★ 5 stars
Sep 2025
Synthesis

Murphy's central premise is that the subconscious mind is a powerful force that responds to belief and intention. Your highlights center on the principle that clearly directed thought and faith activate the subconscious to work toward your goals. The book explores how the mind can be trained to manifest positive outcomes through consistent, intentional mental practice and unwavering belief in desired outcomes.

Key Insights
  • The subconscious responds to clear, faith-driven intention
  • Mental and physical states are deeply interconnected
  • Repeated affirmations and visualization reshape your reality
  • Your beliefs are the blueprints for your life outcomes

The Psychology of Money

Morgan Housel

★★★☆☆ 3 stars
Synthesis

Housel's thesis is that financial success has less to do with intelligence and more to do with behavior. Your highlights paint a vivid picture of his key arguments: wealth is invisible (it's what you don't buy), happiness comes from controlling your time rather than accumulating possessions, and the biggest financial skill is knowing when you have enough. The danger of social comparison runs throughout—moving goalposts, envy, and ego are the real wealth destroyers. Your highlights suggest you were particularly drawn to the idea that true wealth means freedom: the ability to do what you want, when you want, with whom you want.

Key Insights
  • Wealth = what you don't see. It's the cars not purchased, not the ones in the driveway
  • The hardest financial skill is getting the goalpost to stop moving
  • Control over your time pays the highest dividend in finance
  • Savings = income minus ego
  • Volatility is the price of admission for long-term investing success
